## Q1: Most Accessible Buyer Persona for Outbound/Inbound

**Rank: Specialty Insurance (Market 1) > Mental Health (Market 5) > Mid-Market Manufacturing (Market 2)**

### #1 — Specialty Insurance (MGAs)

This is ABM heaven. ~200 MGAs control most of the specialty market — the entire addressable universe fits in a single spreadsheet. The buyer persona is well-defined: Chief Underwriting Officer or Head of Operations at a firm with 50–500 employees. High WTP ($50K–$200K/year) means CAC economics work even at a $30K+ cost-per-close. These buyers attend the same 3–4 conferences (TMPAA Summit, NAPSLO/Wholesale & Specialty Insurance Association). LinkedIn targeting by title + company size is surgical.

**Inbound angle:** "Specialty underwriters are leaving bind time on the table" — data-driven content targeting the pain of losing business to faster competitors. ROI calculators convert well with a financially sophisticated audience.

### #2 — Mental Health (Group Practices & Telehealth)

Clinician burnout is a dominant cultural narrative right now. Practice managers and group practice owners actively search for solutions — strong inbound pull. The buyer (Group Practice Owner, VP Clinical Operations at telehealth platforms) has a clear title and gathers at conferences like MGMA Behavioral Health and Behavioral Health Tech. Content strategy is obvious: "Give your best therapists 15 hours/week back."

The inbound funnel is rich: SEO on "SOAP note automation," "prior auth AI," "clinical documentation." Long-form content will convert organic traffic.

### #3 — Mid-Market Manufacturing

The Supply Chain Director / COO at a $50M–$500M manufacturer is reachable via NetSuite/QuickBooks partner channels and industry trade associations (NAM, MAPI). Post-COVID supply chain anxiety created strong inbound pull. However, persona varies by sub-industry (food, automotive, aerospace) which fragments content strategy and increases CPL.

### Why Government Ranks Last

Government procurement is institutional, not persona-driven. Marketing spend doesn't generate RFPs — relationships and vehicles do. Treat it as a BD motion, not a marketing motion. No outbound/inbound budget should be allocated here in the early phase.

## Q2: Strongest Narrative Differentiation vs. Existing Players

### #1 — Mental Health (Market 5): "Not a chatbot — a clinician multiplier"

The dominant AI narrative in behavioral health is patient-facing: crisis bots, digital therapeutics, app-based CBT. Every VC-funded company is competing on that axis. Positioning as a **clinician productivity tool** is genuine counter-positioning — we're not replacing the clinical relationship, we're protecting it by eliminating the administrative drag that drives burnout and early exit from the field. This is a narrative that providers, professional associations, and state licensing boards will actively endorse. No well-capitalized player owns this position yet.

**Tagline direction:** "AI for clinicians, not patients."

### #2 — Legal SMB (Market 6): "BigLaw gets Harvey. You get nothing."

Harvey, Clio Duo, and the LegalTech VC wave are all targeting enterprise and BigLaw. The 1–20 attorney firm has been explicitly deprioritized. A positioning built on that tension is both true and emotionally resonant for small firm attorneys who already feel abandoned by the legal tech industry. Bar association distribution amplifies this — we'd be endorsed by the institutions that represent them.

**Tagline direction:** "AI that fits a solo practice, not a law firm with 400 attorneys."

### #3 — Construction (Market 3): "Your project manager can't read 40,000 PDFs. Ours can."

Procore and Autodesk own the scheduling and BIM story. No one owns the unstructured document intelligence story — RFIs, change orders, daily reports. The "80% of projects finish late or over budget" stat is a powerful hook that GCs will recognize viscerally. The RFI conflict detection wedge is specific and testable (prospect can immediately validate it against their last project).

## Q3: Distribution Thesis Weaknesses — Reframes

### Legal (Market 6): Platform risk is the fatal flaw in the current thesis

The summary leans heavily on Clio and MyCase as primary distribution. **This is the weakest link.** Both are well-funded and have publicly stated AI roadmaps. Clio Duo already exists. If we build our GTM around being an add-on to platforms that have every incentive to absorb our functionality, we're building on sand.

**Reframe:** Make state bar association endorsement the primary channel, not secondary. State bars have Practice Management Advisory Programs (PMAPs) that recommend tools to solo/small firm attorneys. A bar endorsement reaches 40,000–80,000 attorneys per state without a sales motion and cannot be replicated by Clio. Platform integrations (Clio, MyCase) remain useful as technical connectors but should not be the distribution thesis.

### Mental Health (Market 5): Wrong telehealth targets

The summary targets "telehealth platforms" but the specific companies in that category (Headway, Alma, Grow Therapy) are VC-funded scale-ups that will build clinical documentation AI in-house within 18 months. They have the engineering resources and the incentive.

**Reframe:** Target **employer-sponsored EAP (Employee Assistance Program) networks** (Lyra, Spring Health, ComPsych) and **large health systems** with behavioral health lines of service. Health systems procurement is slower but they won't build in-house. EAP networks have quality and efficiency pressure from employer clients. Neither can easily build this themselves.

### Construction (Market 3): Implementation burden kills unit economics

A $3K–$12K/month direct SaaS with "implementation support" for GCs managing $10M–$200M projects sounds right on price but the implementation motion will destroy margins at scale. GCs are not SaaS buyers — they're relationship buyers who expect hand-holding. A 6-week implementation cycle on a $5K/month contract has brutal payback.

**Reframe:** Prioritize embedded distribution via Procore's App Marketplace and Autodesk Construction Cloud's ecosystem. Procore has 16,000+ customers and a vetted marketplace buyers already trust. Marketplace listing gets us inbound from customers in their existing workflow. Implementation burden drops to configuration. We forfeit some margin on marketplace fees but gain viral adoption and dramatically lower CAC.
