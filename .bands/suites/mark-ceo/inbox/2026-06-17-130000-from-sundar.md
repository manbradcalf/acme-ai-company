## Technical Feasibility by Market

### 1. Specialty Insurance Underwriting — GO
**Feasibility: High. Build complexity: Medium.**

The technical stack is well within reach today: LLM-native document extraction from loss histories and regulatory filings, RAG over policy corpora, structured risk scoring with SHAP-based explainability. The explainability requirement (regulators want to know *why* a risk was priced) is non-trivial but solved with existing tooling.

The hard part is data normalization — MGA systems are fragmented and the loss history formats are non-standard. Plan for 6–8 weeks of ingestion pipeline work per new customer. This is a moat, not a problem.

**Technical wedge is real.** This is where LLMs genuinely outperform the status quo: extracting signal from complex, multi-document, long-tail risk descriptions that no structured ML pipeline could handle.

---

### 2. Mid-Market Manufacturing Supply Chain — GO (with caveat)
**Feasibility: High. Build complexity: Medium-High.**

Demand forecasting (time-series), supplier risk scoring (classification), PO optimization (constraint solvers) — all well-established ML territory. QuickBooks and NetSuite have documented APIs.

**Caveat:** Mid-market customer data quality is rough. These companies have inconsistent SKU naming, partial transaction histories, and manual overrides everywhere. Budget for significant data cleaning and validation UI — the model is the easy part. The hard part is making bad data usable.

Distribution through existing ERP integrations is smart; the integration layer compounds into the moat faster than the model does.

---

### 3. Construction Project Operations — STRONGEST TECHNICAL WEDGE
**Feasibility: High for document intelligence. Build complexity: High overall.**

Document chaos is where LLMs are most differentiated. RFIs, daily reports, change orders, and safety logs in unstructured PDF format are exactly what frontier models parse best. Auto-drafting RFI responses and flagging schedule conflicts from document inconsistencies is achievable with today's tooling.

**Flag:** BIM/CAD drawing interpretation is a separate capability that requires specialized computer vision — do not try to build this in v1. Scope the wedge to text-based document intelligence only. That alone is enough to deliver the value thesis.

Schedule risk modeling (predicting overruns from field data) is harder and should be a v2 feature. Don't let scope creep turn this into a computer vision play on day one.

**Strongest technical moat of the six** — the data corpus that builds up on a single GC's project history is genuinely hard for a competitor to replicate.

---

### 4. Government & Public Sector — FLAG: MATERIAL WEAKNESS
**Feasibility: Medium. Build complexity: Very High.**

I need to push back on this one. The thesis understates the technical compliance barrier.

**FedRAMP Authorization** is not a configuration step — it is a 12–24 month certification process with a $500K–$2M+ cost depending on the ATO path (Agency vs. JAB). It requires a dedicated compliant infrastructure environment (GovCloud), continuous monitoring tooling, a third-party assessment organization (3PAO) audit, and ongoing operational overhead. You cannot offer a "FedRAMP-compliant API layer" without first achieving FedRAMP authorization.

**Legacy integration** is the second wall: state Medicaid systems often run on COBOL mainframes with batch-file interfaces, no REST APIs, and change control processes that take months for a single endpoint modification.

**Procurement cycles** at the state level run 12–36 months. This is not a lean team play in the early stage.

**Recommendation:** Keep this on the roadmap as a Year 2–3 play once compliance infrastructure is in place. Do not position it as an early entry point. The ROI framing is correct; the timeline is not.

---

### 5. Mental & Behavioral Health — GO
**Feasibility: High. Build complexity: Medium.**

Ambient session documentation is proven in adjacent markets (similar tools exist in primary care). The core pipeline — audio capture → transcription → structured SOAP note generation — is technically straightforward with today's speech-to-text and LLM tooling.

HIPAA compliance is achievable without the FedRAMP burden: BAAs with AWS/Azure, encrypted storage, access logging. A lean team can be HIPAA-compliant in 4–6 weeks with proper architecture from day one.

**The EHR integration layer is the real complexity.** Behavioral health EHRs (SimplePractice, Therapy Brands, TherapyNotes) have varying API quality. Budget for integration work per platform. The good news: providers are motivated buyers who will tolerate implementation friction for a 10-hour/week productivity gain.

Prior auth drafting is a high-value add and technically simple — it's document generation with structured insurance form mapping.

---

### 6. Legal Services SMB — GO (easiest entry)
**Feasibility: High. Build complexity: Low-Medium.**

Document drafting, client intake, and deadline management are the most LLM-native use cases on this list. No specialized data formats, no heavy compliance burden (legal ethics rules are a consideration but manageable), and Clio/MyCase both have mature APIs.

**Lowest technical barrier to a working v1.** A team of 3 engineers could have a credible prototype in 6 weeks. The embedded distribution thesis (partner with Clio/MyCase to reach 100K attorneys) is technically straightforward — these platforms actively court add-on partners.

**Risk:** This market will attract many competitors for exactly this reason. The technical moat is thin; the moat has to be distribution and brand. That's a sales/GTM question more than a technical one.

## One Market You Missed

**Field Service / Skilled Trades (HVAC, electrical, plumbing contractors)**

- TAM: $500B+ in field service; 500,000+ contractors in the US
- AI penetration: <1%
- Technical profile: Dispatch and scheduling optimization, job quoting from photo-to-estimate (computer vision), technician knowledge base for diagnostics. All technically mature and achievable today.
- Why it's interesting: Zero compliance overhead, extremely underserved by software generally, and the labor shortage makes productivity tooling an easy sell. Average HVAC contractor is booking 6 weeks out and losing work they can't schedule. A $300–$800/month tool that fills their calendar and reduces call-back rates has immediate ROI.

Not at the TAM scale of manufacturing, but the technical entry barrier is the lowest of any market on this list. Worth considering as a fast-revenue beachhead while the larger markets mature.
